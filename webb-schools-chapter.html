Food Waste Trends

Data Analysis

The Webb Schools' Foodnomics chapter, led by Mary Li, has achieved exceptional results in reducing food waste across both lunch and dinner services. Mary's systematic approach to data collection and student engagement has created one of the most consistent chapters in the Foodnomics network.

Starting in September 2024, The Webb Schools recorded significant food waste levels with lunch averaging $277.75 and dinner averaging $310.90. Through persistent awareness campaigns and strategic use of the Foodnomics app, Mary has led her school to impressive reductions across the full academic year.

Outstanding achievements at The Webb Schools include:

  1. Remarkable lunch improvements: 37.8% reduction from September ($277.75) to May ($172.70)
  2. Consistent dinner progress: 42.2% reduction from September ($310.90) to May ($179.60)
  3. Sustained momentum: Achieved reductions in 8 out of 9 months for both meal periods
  4. Exceptional cost savings: Combined monthly reduction of over $230 in food waste
  5. Leadership excellence: Maintained consistent engagement throughout the full academic year

Mary Li's leadership at The Webb Schools demonstrates how dedicated student leadership can create lasting institutional change while generating significant cost savings and environmental benefits. Her nearly 40% average reduction across both lunch and dinner services represents one of the strongest overall performances in the Foodnomics network.

Foodnomics

At Foodnomics, we recognize a long-standing problem as poverty and hunger, and we are determined to address it with the next generation of the world's leaders. The world produces more than enough food to feed its people, yet millions of people are still hungry, struggling to find their next meal. A contributing factor to this problem is waste. All across America, 60 million tons of food are being discarded each year, This is especially prevalent in dining halls across the country. But together, we can lessen this problem. By founding a chapter at your school and using the resources that we provide, you can collect data on how much food is being wasted and its monetary value, then work with your dining hall staff to take appropriate measures to fix the problem by saving both money and resources. By influencing your immediate community, you are helping your locality, but collectively we can take on the world's most important problem, one meal at a time.
